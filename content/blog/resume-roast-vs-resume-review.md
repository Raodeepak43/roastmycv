## Real example: same CV, two outputs

Imagine this bullet on a BTech fresher resume:

> *"Worked on various projects and gained good knowledge of Java and teamwork."*

**Typical resume review output:**
- "Add more keywords"
- "Quantify achievements"
- Score: 62/100

**Resume roast output (MyCVRoast):**
- Quotes the exact line
- Calls out "various projects" with no names, stack, or outcome
- Suggests a rewrite: *"Built attendance app (Java, Firebase) used by 120 students — reduced manual entry by 4 hrs/week"*
- Gives a score out of 10 with tone you chose (clean / Hinglish / savage)

That gap — generic vs line-level — is why Indian freshers often feel reviews "say nothing new" while roasts actually change what they send.

## India-specific: why Hinglish roast matters

Indian recruiters and seniors often give feedback in mixed Hindi-English. A roast in **natural Hinglish** mirrors how campus placement cells and IT managers actually talk:

- *"Bhai objective section 2010 ka lag raha hai"*
- *"Skills mein MS Word mat likho — kuch unique dikhao"*
- *"Projects blank hain, CGPA hide kiya — recruiter kya dekhega?"*

Generic English-only reviews miss this cultural context. If you are applying through Naukri, LinkedIn, or campus drives in Tier-2/Tier-3 cities, tone and clarity matter as much as keywords.

## After the roast: what to fix first

Priority order that works for most Indian freshers:

1. **Summary / objective** — delete template lines; lead with role + stack + outcome.
2. **Project bullets** — name the project, tech, metric (users, %, time saved).
3. **Skills** — remove filler (MS Word, "internet"); keep defensible skills.
4. **Length** — one page for &lt;3 years experience. See [how many pages should a resume be](/blog/how-many-pages-resume).
5. **Format** — single column, PDF, clean filename. See [CV format for freshers](/blog/cv-format-for-freshers).

Then re-roast. If score jumps 2+ points, you are ready to apply.
