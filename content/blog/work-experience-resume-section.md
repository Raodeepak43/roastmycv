The **work experience resume** section is where most candidates win or lose the shortlist. Recruiters spend their precious 6–10 seconds here, scanning for proof that you delivered results — not a list of duties copied from a job description. The difference between a resume that gets a callback and one that gets ignored often comes down to how you write these bullets. This guide shows you how to write work experience in a CV using resume experience examples and the impact-first formula that actually works in 2026.

If your experience section reads like your offer letter's responsibilities, it is time for a rewrite.

## Duties vs. Impact: The Core Difference

Almost every weak work experience resume section makes the same mistake: it describes what you were *supposed* to do instead of what you *achieved*.

- **Duty-focused (weak):** *Responsible for managing social media accounts.*
- **Impact-focused (strong):** *Grew the company's LinkedIn following from 2k to 11k in 8 months, generating 30+ qualified leads through organic content.*

Recruiters already know what a social media manager does. What they want to know is how *well* you did it. Every bullet should answer "so what?" with a result, a number, or a clear outcome.

## The Formula: Action Verb + Task + Result

The most reliable structure for professional experience examples is:

**[Strong action verb] + [what you did] + [measurable result].**

Examples:

- *Automated weekly reporting (Python, SQL), cutting manual effort from 6 hours to 30 minutes per week.*
- *Led a 4-person team to migrate 12 legacy modules to React, reducing page load time by 40%.*
- *Negotiated with 5 vendors to lower procurement costs by ₹8 lakh annually.*

Start strong. A flat verb like "handled" or "worked on" wastes your most valuable position in the bullet. Our [resume action verbs](/blog/resume-action-verbs) list gives you replacements that punch.

## How to Quantify When You Don't Have Obvious Numbers

"But my job didn't have metrics!" Almost every role does — you just have to look. Sources of numbers:

- **Scale:** team size, number of clients, tickets handled, records processed
- **Time:** hours saved, faster turnaround, deadlines met
- **Money:** revenue influenced, costs reduced, budget managed
- **Quality:** error rate reduced, satisfaction/feedback scores
- **Frequency:** reports per week, releases per month

If exact numbers are unavailable, use honest estimates or ranges ("supported ~50 daily users," "handled 20+ tickets/day"). Never fabricate — you must defend every claim in the interview.

## Structuring Each Role

For each position in your work experience resume section, include:

1. **Job title** (bold)
2. **Company name and location**
3. **Dates** (month/year — month/year)
4. **3–5 bullets** in impact-first format

Order roles in reverse chronological order (most recent first). Give your current/most relevant role the most bullets; trim older roles to 1–2 lines.

### Work Experience Resume Example (Experienced)

- **Senior Data Analyst** — FinEdge Pvt Ltd, Bengaluru (Jun 2023 – Present)
  - *Built 9 automated Power BI dashboards adopted by 4 business teams, saving ~20 analyst hours/week.*
  - *Identified a billing leakage pattern in transaction data that recovered ₹12 lakh in the first quarter.*
  - *Mentored 2 junior analysts, both promoted within a year.*

This is concise, specific, and result-driven — the hallmark of strong work experience resume examples.

## Writing Work Experience in a CV With Little History

If you only have an internship or two, the same rules apply — describe the real work and outcomes, not the title. A short but strong experience section beats a padded one.

- *Operations Intern (3 months) — streamlined the delivery tracking sheet, reducing daily reconciliation time by 45 minutes.*

## Tailoring Experience to the Job

A generic experience section ranks lower on keyword match and feels irrelevant. For each application:

- Surface the bullets most relevant to the target role
- Mirror key terms from the job description (honestly)
- Cut or shorten bullets that do not support the role

This light tailoring matters because ATS and recruiters both reward relevance. To understand the screening logic, read our [ATS-friendly resume](/blog/ats-friendly-resume) guide.

## Common Work Experience Mistakes

Avoid these patterns that sink experience sections:

1. **Copy-pasting the job description** as your bullets
2. **Paragraphs instead of bullets** — nobody reads dense blocks
3. **No numbers anywhere** — every bullet sounds the same
4. **Listing every task** instead of the 3–5 that matter
5. **Inconsistent tense** — use past tense for previous roles, present for current
6. **Vague verbs** like "handled," "involved in," "worked on"

## FAQ

### How do I write the work experience section of a resume?

Use the formula: strong action verb + what you did + measurable result. Write 3–5 impact bullets per role, in reverse chronological order, and quantify wherever possible.

### What if I have no measurable achievements?

Look harder — scale, time saved, costs, quality, and frequency are all measurable. Use honest estimates if exact figures are unavailable, but never invent numbers you cannot defend.

### How many bullets per job should I use?

Three to five for recent and relevant roles; one to two for older or less relevant ones. Quality and relevance beat quantity.

### Should I include every job I've ever had?

No. Focus on relevant and recent roles. Brief, ancient, or unrelated jobs can be summarized or omitted, especially as your career grows.
