A **simple resume** almost always beats a flashy one — and not because recruiters are boring. Clean, single-column layouts parse correctly through Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS), load fast on a recruiter's phone, and let your actual content do the talking. The fancy template with sidebars, icons, and three colors? It often turns into scrambled text the moment software reads it. This guide shows you a reliable simple resume format with basic resume examples you can adapt today.

If your applications keep disappearing into a void, the problem may not be your experience — it may be a layout the machine cannot read.

## Why Simple Wins in 2026

Indian hiring runs on volume. A single fresher role can attract hundreds of applications, and almost every large employer (TCS, Infosys, Wipro, Amazon India, Flipkart) screens with ATS before a human ever looks. A basic resume format helps because:

- **It parses cleanly** — no tables, text boxes, or columns to confuse the software
- **It is fast to scan** — recruiters spend 6–10 seconds on the first pass
- **It travels well** — opens correctly across Word, PDF viewers, and phones
- **It looks professional** — clean signals competence, clutter signals panic

## The Simple Resume Format Structure

Here is the order that works for almost everyone. Keep it single-column, top to bottom:

1. **Header** — name, city, phone, professional email, LinkedIn (and GitHub for tech roles)
2. **Summary** — 2–3 lines: who you are, target role, one proof point
3. **Experience or Projects** — reverse chronological, impact bullets
4. **Skills** — grouped logically, honest
5. **Education** — degree, institution, year, CGPA if strong
6. **Extras** — certifications, achievements (only if relevant)

That is it. No "Declaration," no photo, no date of birth, no signature line. Those belong to a different era.

## Basic Resume Example (Fresher)

Here is what a simple resume example looks like in plain structure:

- **Name** — City · Phone · email · LinkedIn · GitHub
- **Summary:** *B.Com graduate (2026) targeting accounts/finance roles; comfortable with Tally, Excel, and GST basics through a 3-month internship.*
- **Projects/Internship:** 2–3 bullets each with outcomes
- **Skills:** Accounting (Tally, Excel), Finance basics (GST, TDS), Tools (MS Office)
- **Education:** B.Com, University name, 2026, CGPA 8.1

## Formatting Rules for a Clean Look

A simple resume is not a plain-text dump. Small choices make it look polished:

- **Font:** one clean font (Calibri, Arial, or Georgia), size 10–12 for body
- **Headings:** bold and slightly larger, consistent throughout
- **Margins:** 0.5"–1" — do not cram or leave huge gaps
- **Spacing:** consistent gaps between sections
- **Bullets:** standard round bullets, not arrows or emojis
- **File:** save as PDF unless the portal asks for .docx; name it `YourName_Resume.pdf`

Avoid: text boxes, two-column layouts, header/footer contact info (ATS often skips these), and tables.

## Before and After: Simple Beats Busy

The same content reads completely differently depending on formatting.

**Before (cluttered bullet inside a two-column box):**

- *Worked on sales · achieved targets · handled customers · did reporting*

**After (clean single-column bullet):**

- *Exceeded monthly sales target by 18% for 6 consecutive months while managing a 40-customer portfolio in the Pune region.*

## Simple Resume Format for Different Situations

### For Freshers and Students

Lead with projects and education. Keep it to one page. A simple layout signals that you respect the recruiter's time. Our [student resume guide](/blog/student-resume-guide) covers what to include when experience is limited.

### For Experienced Professionals

Lead with work experience and impact metrics. Education shrinks to one or two lines. Even at 5+ years, simple still wins — clutter reads as disorganization.

### For Career Changers

Use a simple format with a short summary explaining the pivot, then a skills section that maps transferable strengths to the new role.

## Common Mistakes That Kill Simple Resumes

Even a basic resume can go wrong. Watch for:

1. **Walls of text** — break duties into 3–5 crisp bullets per role
2. **Inconsistent formatting** — mismatched fonts, sizes, or bullet styles
3. **Skill bars and star ratings** — they mean nothing to a recruiter
4. **Stuffing one page into a tiny font** — trim content instead
5. **Generic objective lines** — replace with a real summary

## FAQ

### Is a simple resume format good enough for big companies?

Yes — in fact it is better. Large companies rely heavily on ATS, and simple single-column formats parse most reliably. Save the creativity for your portfolio, not your CV layout.

### Should a simple resume be one page or two?

One page for freshers and most candidates under ~7 years of experience. Two pages only if you have genuine substance to fill it.

### Can I use a free template for a basic resume?

Yes, but choose plain, single-column templates. Avoid heavily designed Canva-style files with sidebars and icons — they often break when software reads them.

### Does a simple resume look unprofessional?

The opposite. Clean and well-organized reads as confident and professional. Clutter reads as someone hiding thin content behind decoration.
